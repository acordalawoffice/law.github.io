<h1>A job offer you sign is a job you already have</h1>
<p class="subtitle">What the Aragones v. Alltech ruling means for Filipino workers and employers &mdash; G.R. No. 251736, decided April 2, 2025, Supreme Court of the Philippines</p>
<div class="article-meta" style="font-size: 0.85rem; color: #718096; margin-bottom: 20px;">Published on July 18, 2026 • Legal Educational Article</div>

<p>When does an employee actually become an employee? Most of us would assume it's on the first day of work &mdash; when you clock in, sign the payroll forms, and settle into your desk. But in a landmark decision, the Philippine Supreme Court said otherwise: the moment you sign and return a job offer, the employer-employee relationship already exists, even if your first day is still months away.</p>

<p>The ruling, handed down in <em>Paolo Landayan Aragones v. Alltech Biotechnology Corporation</em> (G.R. No. 251736), is already being called one of the most consequential labor law decisions of the past few years &mdash; and it carries real implications for how companies handle hiring, and how workers understand their rights the moment they say "yes."</p>

<h2>What happened</h2>
<p>In April 2016, Alltech Biotechnology Corporation offered Paolo Aragones the position of Swine Technical Manager &ndash; Pacific. Aragones signed and returned the offer letter on April 18, 2016, accepting a role set to begin on July 1, 2016. Trusting the offer was final, he resigned from his existing job at Cargill Philippines just days later.</p>
<p>But before his start date arrived, Alltech's head office announced a global restructuring. The company informed Aragones that his position had been abolished due to redundancy, and offered him roughly one month's salary as a "goodwill" gesture. Aragones didn't accept it &mdash; he filed a labor complaint instead.</p>
<p>Alltech's defense hinged on a technicality: it argued that no employer-employee relationship had ever existed, since Aragones hadn't started working, hadn't signed a formal employment contract yet, and his own offer letter listed July 1 as the "commencement" date.</p>

<h2>Timeline</h2>
<ul>
  <li><strong>April 1, 2016</strong> &mdash; Alltech offers Aragones the role, with a start date of July 1.</li>
  <li><strong>April 18, 2016</strong> &mdash; Aragones signs and returns the offer. The Court later rules this is when the contract was perfected.</li>
  <li><strong>June 10, 2016</strong> &mdash; Alltech withdraws the offer before the start date, citing redundancy.</li>
  <li><strong>April 2, 2025</strong> &mdash; The Supreme Court rules the dismissal illegal and orders backwages and separation pay.</li>
</ul>

<h2>What the Supreme Court decided</h2>
<p>The Court rejected Alltech's argument outright. It held that an employment contract &mdash; like any other contract &mdash; is perfected the moment there is a clear offer, an acceptance, and communication of that acceptance back to the employer. That happened on April 18, when Aragones signed and returned the offer letter.</p>

<blockquote>The July 1 start date wasn't a condition that had to be met before the relationship could exist &mdash; it was simply a period, a scheduled point in time that deferred when Aragones had to report for work and when Alltech had to start paying him. The relationship itself was already in place.</blockquote>

<p>Because Alltech couldn't back up its redundancy claim with real evidence &mdash; it submitted only a vague affidavit from a company officer, with no staffing plan, cost analysis, or documentation showing the position was genuinely eliminated &mdash; the Court ruled that Aragones had been illegally dismissed. He was awarded backwages from his intended start date, separation pay in lieu of reinstatement, attorney's fees, and legal interest.</p>

<h2>Why this matters</h2>
<p>The ruling reshapes how offer letters should be read and drafted going forward, for both sides of the hiring table.</p>
<p><strong>For employees:</strong> Once you sign a job offer, you may already have enforceable rights &mdash; including protection against being dropped without valid cause and due process. A verbal "we'll finalize everything on day one" doesn't erase what already happened the moment you accepted in writing.</p>
<p><strong>For employers:</strong> Offer letters are no longer just formalities &mdash; they bind the moment they're signed. Companies that want flexibility to withdraw an offer before a start date need to build that in explicitly, and any redundancy claim must be backed by real, documented business reasons.</p>

<h2>The bigger picture</h2>
<p>The Aragones case reinforces a theme running through Philippine labor jurisprudence: the law leans toward protecting the worker, especially in the imbalance of power between someone accepting a job and the company offering it. It's a reminder that in the Philippines, "you're hired" can carry more legal weight than either side may realize &mdash; starting the moment the ink dries, not the moment the desk chair is warmed.</p>
