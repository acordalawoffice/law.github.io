    <h3 style="font-size: 2.0rem; margin-top: 0; line-height: 1.3;">Project Employment: Statutory Controls and the Danger of Automatic Regularization</h3>
    <div class="article-meta" style="color: #718096; font-size: 0.9rem; margin-bottom: 15px; line-height: 1.4;">Published: July 2026 | Category: Employment Status</div>

    <p style="text-align: justify; line-height: 1.75; margin-bottom: 22px;">
        Article 295 of the Labor Code allows for <strong>Project Employment</strong> as an explicit statutory exception to regular employment. A project employee is defined as a worker whose employment has been fixed for a specific project or undertaking, the completion or termination of which has been <strong>determined at the time of the engagement</strong>. This is highly relevant in construction, filmmaking, and specialized software development.
    </p>

    <h4 style="font-size: 1.3rem; line-height: 1.3; margin-top: 25px; margin-bottom: 12px;">The Strict Prerequisites to Prove Valid Project Status</h4>
    <p style="text-align: justify; line-height: 1.75; margin-bottom: 14px;">
        To defend against claims of illegal dismissal and automatic regular employment status, the employer bears the burden of proving two concurrent operational facts:
    </p>
    <ol style="padding-left: 20px; margin-bottom: 22px; color: #333; text-align: justify; line-height: 1.65;">
        <li style="margin-bottom: 12px;">The project, scope, and specific timeline must be explicitly detailed in a written <strong>Project Employment Contract</strong> signed by the employee <em>at the very start</em> of the engagement.</li>
        <li style="margin-bottom: 12px;">The employer must submit a formal <strong>Establishment Report of Project Termination</strong> to the nearest DOLE provincial office within fifteen (15) days of the completion of each project. Failure to file this report is treated by the NLRC as clear evidence that the worker is a regular employee, not a project hand.</li>
    </ol>

    <h4 style="font-size: 1.3rem; line-height: 1.3; margin-top: 25px; margin-bottom: 12px;">The Effect of Continuous Rehiring and Length of Service</h4>
    <p style="text-align: justify; line-height: 1.75; margin-bottom: 18px;">
        While the Supreme Court recognizes that project employees can be rehired for new, separate projects, <strong>repeated and continuous rehiring over a prolonged duration</strong> can transform a project worker into a regular employee by operation of law. 
    </p>
    <p style="text-align: justify; line-height: 1.75; margin-bottom: 18px;">
        This occurs when the tasks performed by the project employee are <strong>vital, necessary, and indispensable</strong> to the usual business or trade of the employer. If a worker continuously jumps from project to project for years doing the exact same core work without actual gaps in employment, the law strips away the "project" label to prevent the abuse of fixed-term contracts. They achieve regular status, meaning they can no longer be separated without a valid Just or Authorized cause and full due process.
    </p>
