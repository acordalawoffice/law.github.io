    <h3 style="font-size: 2.0rem; color: #1a202c; margin-top: 0; margin-bottom: 10px;">Just Causes for Termination: Gross and Habitual Neglect of Duties</h3>
    <div class="article-meta" style="font-size: 0.85rem; color: #718096; margin-bottom: 20px;">Published: July 2026 | Category: Disciplinary Prerogatives</div>

    <div class="article-body" style="color: #2d3748; line-height: 1.7; text-align: justify;">
        <p style="margin-bottom: 15px;">
            Under Article 297 (b) of the Labor Code, an employee can be lawfully dismissed if they are guilty of <strong>Gross and Habitual Neglect</strong> of their assigned corporate duties. This ground addresses persistent operational failures, chronic absenteeism, and severe carelessness.
        </p>
        <p style="margin-bottom: 15px;">
            To satisfy the legal standard, the neglect must possess two distinct characteristics simultaneously:
        </p>
        <p style="margin-bottom: 15px;">
            First, the neglect must be <strong>gross</strong>. This means there is an absence of care or a blatant failure to exercise even slight diligence. It indicates a reckless disregard for the consequences of one's actions on company operations or safety. 
        </p>
        <p style="margin-bottom: 20px;">
            Second, the neglect must be <strong>habitual</strong>. The failure cannot be an isolated incident or a one-time oversight. It must consist of repeated, recurring omissions or infractions over a period of time (e.g., a long sequence of unexcused tardiness, or repeatedly failing to perform mandatory security system overrides despite explicit warnings).
        </p>

        <blockquote style="margin: 25px 0; padding: 15px 20px; background: #fdfaf2; border-left: 4px solid #D4AF37; font-style: italic; color: #444;">
            <strong>The Single Infraction Exception:</strong> While habituality is generally required, the Supreme Court has carved out rare exceptions where a <em>single instance</em> of gross neglect can justify termination—specifically if that single act causes immense, irreparable damage, massive financial loss, or puts human lives at immediate risk.
        </blockquote>
    </div>
