<h1>Can your employer stop you from working for a competitor?</h1>
<p class="subtitle">Non-compete agreements under Philippine law &mdash; what makes them valid, what makes them void, and what both employers and employees should know</p>
<div class="article-meta" style="font-size: 0.85rem; color: #718096; margin-bottom: 20px;">Published on July 18, 2026 • Legal Educational Article</div>

<p>You resign, land a better offer from a rival company, and are about to start &mdash; only for your former employer to remind you that you signed a non-compete clause. Can they actually stop you? In the Philippines, the answer is rarely a flat yes or no. It depends on how the clause was written, how it's being enforced, and whether it passes a test the courts have built up over decades of case law.</p>

<h2>There's no specific law against them</h2>
<p>Unlike some countries that have banned or heavily restricted non-compete clauses, the Philippines has no statute that outlaws them outright. Non-compete agreements here are treated as a matter of ordinary contract law, not labor law, which is why disputes over them are generally filed in the regular courts rather than before labor tribunals.</p>
<p>Their legal foundation is Article 1306 of the Civil Code, which allows contracting parties to establish whatever stipulations, clauses, terms, and conditions they deem convenient &mdash; provided these are not contrary to law, morals, good customs, public order, or public policy. A non-compete clause is enforceable only to the extent it survives that last qualifier.</p>

<h2>The reasonableness test</h2>
<p>Because the Constitution protects the right to work and public policy favors free competition and employee mobility, Philippine courts don't enforce non-compete clauses automatically just because they were signed. They weigh them against a multi-factor reasonableness test, drawn from cases such as <em>Rivera v. Solidbank Corporation</em>. A clause is more likely to hold up if it can answer yes to each of the following:</p>
<ul>
  <li>Does it protect a legitimate business interest of the employer, such as trade secrets, client relationships, or specialized training?</li>
  <li>Does it avoid placing an undue burden on the employee's ability to earn a living?</li>
  <li>Is it not injurious to public welfare or competition generally?</li>
  <li>Are its time and territorial limitations reasonable?</li>
  <li>Is the overall restraint reasonable from the standpoint of public policy?</li>
</ul>
<p>These factors are applied case by case. A clause that is too broad in scope, too long in duration, or too wide in geographic reach is more likely to be struck down as an unreasonable restraint of trade.</p>

<h2>The leading case: Tiu v. Platinum Plans</h2>
<p>The clearest illustration of how this test plays out is <em>Daisy B. Tiu v. Platinum Plans Phil., Inc.</em> (G.R. No. 163512, February 28, 2007). Tiu, a senior executive with access to confidential marketing strategies, signed a five-year employment contract containing a clause barring her from engaging in any competing pre-need business for two years after separation, with liquidated damages of P100,000 for breach. She left and joined a rival pre-need company within that two-year window.</p>

<blockquote>A non-involvement or non-compete clause in an employment contract is not per se void for being in restraint of trade, provided it is reasonable as to time, trade, and place, and is not greater than necessary to afford fair and reasonable protection to the employer.</blockquote>

<p>The Supreme Court upheld the clause and ordered Tiu to pay the stipulated damages. The two-year limit was reasonable, the restriction was narrowly tied to the same industry she had worked in, and her senior role gave her real access to sensitive company information &mdash; exactly the kind of legitimate interest a non-compete clause is meant to protect.</p>

<h2>What tends to weaken a non-compete clause</h2>
<p>Not every clause fares as well as the one in <em>Tiu</em>. Employers carry the burden of proving both the clause's validity and the reasonableness of its restrictions &mdash; simply pointing to a competing job is not enough. A few situations tend to work against enforcement:</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Involuntary termination.</strong> Courts increasingly question whether it's fair to restrict someone's next job when they didn't choose to leave in the first place, such as in a layoff or redundancy.</li>
  <li><strong>Overly broad restrictions.</strong> A clause covering an entire industry nationwide, or lasting far longer than needed to protect a legitimate interest, is more vulnerable to being struck down.</li>
  <li><strong>Rank-and-file employees with no real access to sensitive information.</strong> The less a role resembles Tiu's &mdash; senior, strategic, information-rich &mdash; the harder it is to justify the restriction.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Practical guidance</h2>
<p><strong>For employers:</strong> Draft narrowly. Tie the clause to a specific, identifiable interest, keep the time and geographic scope proportionate, and be ready to show real evidence of harm or risk if you ever need to enforce it. In many cases, a well-drafted non-disclosure or non-solicitation agreement protects the same interests with far less legal risk than an outright non-compete.</p>
<p><strong>For employees:</strong> Read the clause before you sign, not after you resign. Ask what business interest it's protecting, whether the time and place restrictions are reasonable, and whether you're being asked to give up more than the company genuinely needs. If you're ever let go involuntarily, the enforceability of a non-compete you signed becomes considerably weaker.</p>

<h2>The bigger picture</h2>
<p>Philippine jurisprudence on non-compete clauses remains less developed than in jurisdictions with dedicated legislation, which means courts continue to decide these disputes case by case rather than through a single bright-line rule. That gives both employers and employees room to negotiate reasonable terms &mdash; but it also means a poorly drafted clause, on either side, can end up costing far more in a courtroom than it would have taken to get right at the drafting table.</p>
