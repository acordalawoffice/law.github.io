        <h3 style="font-size: 2.0rem; color: #1a202c; margin-top: 0; margin-bottom: 10px;">Understanding "Constructive Dismissal": When Resignation Counts as Termination</h3>
        <div class="article-meta" style="font-size: 0.85rem; color: #718096; margin-bottom: 20px;">Published by Office of Atty. Rodel P. Acorda • Legal Educational Article</div>
        
        <div class="article-body" style="color: #2d3748; line-height: 1.7; text-align: justify;">
            <p style="margin-bottom: 15px;">Under Philippine labor law, security of tenure protects workers from arbitrary termination. While an outright dismissal by an employer is easy to identify, the law also recognizes a more subtle form of termination known as Constructive Dismissal.</p>
            <p style="margin-bottom: 20px;">Often referred to colloquially as an "involuntary resignation," constructive dismissal occurs when an employer creates an environment so hostile, unbearable, or unfavorable that an employee is effectively forced to walk away from their job.</p>

            <h4 style="font-size: 1.15rem; color: #2d3748; margin-top: 25px; margin-bottom: 12px;">The Legal Definition</h4>
            <p style="margin-bottom: 15px;">The Supreme Court of the Philippines defines constructive dismissal as a cessation of work because continued employment is rendered impossible, unreasonable, or unlikely. It involves a clear demotion in rank or a sharp diminution (reduction) in pay, benefits, or privileges.</p>
            <p style="margin-bottom: 20px;">The core test applied by the courts is whether a reasonable person in the employee’s position would feel compelled to give up their employment under the same circumstances.</p>

            <h4 style="font-size: 1.15rem; color: #2d3748; margin-top: 25px; margin-bottom: 12px;">The Legal Reality of "Floating Status"</h4>
            <p style="margin-bottom: 20px;">In specific sectors, such as BPOs and security agencies, employees may temporarily be placed on an off-detail or "floating status" when a client contract terminates. While legally permissible, this status cannot exceed a maximum period of six (6) continuous months. If an employer fails to reassign the employee within six months, the floating status automatically matures into a constructive dismissal by operation of law.</p>

            <h4 style="font-size: 1.15rem; color: #2d3748; margin-top: 25px; margin-bottom: 12px;">The Legal Burden of Proof</h4>
            <p style="margin-bottom: 15px;">In legal disputes before the Labor Arbiter or the National Labor Relations Commission (NLRC), the burden of proof shifts cleanly:</p>
            <ol style="padding-left: 24px; margin-bottom: 0;">
                <li style="margin-bottom: 8px;">The employer must first prove that the employee resigned voluntarily by presenting a clear, uncoerced resignation letter.</li>
                <li style="margin-bottom: 8px;">Once voluntary resignation is argued, the burden shifts to the employee to prove with substantial evidence that their resignation was forced by intolerable conditions created by management.</li>
            </ol>
